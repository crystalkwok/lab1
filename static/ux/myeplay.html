Meet Bob Veillette: a man who has Locked-in Syndrome which leaves him completely paralyzed except for his eyes. Our team had been tasked with creating a working application geared towards people with LIS, and more specifically, Bob. Loved ones usually only have a one-way communication with people with LIS. LIS patients have also communicated that they feel trapped in a limiting body, because they can still hear and be cognizant of the world around them, and their ability to think has not been damaged. Also, people with LIS often find themseves in boredom because of their lack of ability to do a variety of activities. Our eye-tracking based entertainment suite includes games, which can be played as a one or two player, and a media player that utilizes the YouTube API. Our main goals for MyePlay is for Bob's life to be enriched by relieving his boredom, socially interact with his loved ones, and enlightening his mind through videos.

DURATION:   September 2014 - December 2014

ROLE:   UX / UI / Interaction Designer

TOOLS:   Adobe Creative Suite, Wireframing, Prototyping, User Stories/ Scenarios, NodeJS, HTML/CSS

DESCRIPTION:   eye tracking based entertainment suite

COLLABORATORS:   Alex Yang, Andrew Wang, Danny Zhang, Kane Chong, Luke Kim, Sam Suen, Sean Burke, Pavan Kumar

FIRST PROTOTYPE

Being the only designer on the team, I was in charge of designing the user experience, user interface, and interaction of the entertainment suite, although I mostly worked on the menu. Designing for people with LIS has been a completely new experience, considering that the interactions with the technology would be very specialized and limited, especially with Bob's limited action of vertical eye movements only and the limitation of a cheaper eyetracker. Having the chance to design for our users has created new possibilities for interfaces, user experiences, interactions, and even new discoveries to what is intuitive specifically to these users. I created a demo video using Adobe Illustrator that highlights the UI and interactions to show our investors at the Moxie Center. For my design, I chose to make it a vertical spiral to suit Bob, to make it intuitive that this menu was a continuous cycle and can be accessed through up and down movements, and to make only one option available at a time to avoid errors that can be made by the system. In general, my goal was to make the user interface as intuitive and as simple as possible to keep the number of errors our user could make to a minimum.

FINAL WORKING PROTOTYPE
[ more info ]
I was able to see my designs come to life, thanks to the software engineers in the team. After extensive testing with the EyeTribe (the eye tracker we were using), we decided to only have a vertical spiral design, because it would only show one option at once, preventing errors that could easily be made by the system with multiple options available at once. This project opened up new possibilities to rethink already existing functions and purposes of the different parts of the people's bodies, because people with LIS must learn to use their eyes for more purposes than people without LIS usually need to. We feel privileged to have designed something to improve and enrich Bob's life, and to have redefined the eye's purpose and function as not just a passive way to indicate attention, but as an active decision maker.
